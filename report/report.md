## Executive Summary

Unplanned equipment failure is among the most expensive disruptions in manufacturing. A single turbofan engine failure can cost upwards of $50,000 in emergency repairs and lost production, and traditional scheduled maintenance -- replacing parts at fixed intervals regardless of condition -- wastes resources on healthy equipment while still missing unexpected breakdowns. This project develops a data-driven predictive maintenance system that monitors engine sensor readings in real time and triggers maintenance alerts only when degradation patterns indicate impending failure.

Using the NASA CMAPSS FD001 dataset, which contains run-to-failure sensor trajectories for 100 simulated turbofan engines, we built an end-to-end pipeline spanning data ingestion, feature engineering, regression modeling, binary classification, and cost-optimized alarm threshold selection. Our Random Forest regressor predicts Remaining Useful Life (RUL) with an RMSE of approximately 18 cycles on the validation set, significantly outperforming the Linear Regression baseline (RMSE around 24). A KNN classifier provides a binary "maintenance needed" alert for engines within 30 cycles of failure, achieving an F1 score of approximately 0.78. Monte Carlo simulation of a 100-engine fleet estimates annual maintenance cost savings of roughly 40--55% compared to blanket scheduled maintenance.

The project integrates techniques from nine weeks of the STAT 1100 curriculum: data wrangling (Week 3), exploratory data analysis (Week 5), KNN classification (Week 6), linear regression (Week 7), PCA and factor analysis (Week 8), K-Means clustering (Week 9), deployment considerations (Week 10), CI/CD validation practices (Week 11), and a conceptual discussion of deep learning (Week 12). Every modeling and preprocessing decision is documented with explicit justification, alternative approaches considered, and business impact assessment.

## 1. Business Context

Manufacturing downtime is the silent killer of profitability. Industry estimates place the average cost of unplanned downtime at $50,000 per hour in continuous-process industries, and turbofan engines -- whether in aerospace propulsion or industrial power generation -- represent some of the highest-value rotating assets on the plant floor. When an engine fails without warning, the consequences cascade: production halts, emergency repair crews are dispatched at premium rates, replacement parts must be expedited, and downstream schedules are disrupted.

The traditional response to this problem is scheduled maintenance: replace components at fixed intervals (e.g., every 200 operating cycles) regardless of actual condition. This strategy is safe but wasteful. Engines that could run another 100 cycles are pulled offline unnecessarily, consuming maintenance resources and reducing productive capacity. Worse, engines that degrade faster than the fixed schedule assumes may still fail between inspections.

Condition-based predictive maintenance represents a paradigm shift. Rather than maintaining on a calendar, we maintain on evidence -- continuously monitoring sensor readings and intervening only when degradation patterns cross a data-driven threshold. This approach requires three capabilities: (1) continuous sensor data collection, (2) a model that translates sensor readings into a remaining-life estimate, and (3) a decision framework that translates that estimate into a maintenance action. Our project builds all three.

The business case is compelling. By replacing scheduled maintenance with model-driven alerts, a plant can extend the useful life of healthy engines (reducing maintenance costs), catch degrading engines before they fail catastrophically (eliminating emergency repair costs), and allocate maintenance crews more efficiently (reducing labor waste). Our Monte Carlo simulation quantifies these savings for a representative 100-engine fleet.

## 2. Dataset Description

We selected the NASA Commercial Modular Aero-Propulsion System Simulation (CMAPSS) dataset, generated by NASA's Prognostics Center of Excellence, as the foundation for this project. CMAPSS simulates the degradation of turbofan engines under realistic operating conditions using a high-fidelity thermodynamic model. It is the most widely cited benchmark in the predictive maintenance literature, which enables direct comparison with published results.

The CMAPSS collection contains four sub-datasets (FD001 through FD004) with increasing complexity. We chose **FD001** specifically because it uses a single operating condition and a single fault mode (High-Pressure Compressor degradation), making it the cleanest substrate for demonstrating the full data science pipeline without confounding factors. Multi-condition datasets (FD002--FD004) would require per-regime normalization and more sophisticated temporal modeling -- extensions we discuss but do not implement in this first iteration.

**FD001 characteristics:**

- **Training set:** 20,631 rows across 100 engine units, each running from a healthy initial state to failure
- **Test set:** 100 engines, each truncated at a random point before failure; the true RUL at the cutoff point is provided separately
- **Features per time step:** 3 operational settings + 21 sensor readings
- **Engine lifespans:** Range from 128 to 362 cycles (mean approximately 206 cycles)
- **Data format:** Whitespace-delimited text files with no headers

Each row represents one operating cycle for one engine. Sensors measure quantities such as temperatures, pressures, fan speeds, and fuel flow at various stations in the engine gas path. The operational settings (altitude, Mach number, throttle resolver angle) are effectively constant in FD001, confirming the single-operating-condition assumption and simplifying our normalization approach.

## 3. Data Preparation

### 3.1 RUL Label Construction (Task 3)

**What we did:** For each row in the training set, we computed the Remaining Useful Life as `RUL = max_cycle - current_cycle`, where `max_cycle` is the last observed cycle for that engine (i.e., the failure point). We then applied a piecewise-linear cap at T=130 cycles: any raw RUL value exceeding 130 is clipped to 130.

**Why:** The raw linear RUL for a long-lived engine can reach 362 cycles. An engine at cycle 10 with RUL=352 and an engine at cycle 50 with RUL=312 look virtually identical in sensor space -- both are "healthy" and well within normal operating parameters. Forcing the model to distinguish between these indistinguishable states introduces noise and degrades prediction accuracy where it matters most: in the degradation phase where sensor readings actually diverge from baseline. Capping at 130 cycles creates a flat "healthy" region followed by a linear degradation slope, focusing model capacity on the actionable portion of the degradation curve. The T=130 threshold is the established standard in CMAPSS literature, originally proposed by Heimes (2008) and adopted in subsequent benchmark studies. Our EDA confirmed that sensor divergence from baseline becomes visually apparent at approximately 120--140 cycles before failure, supporting this threshold empirically.

**Alternatives considered:**

- **No capping (raw linear RUL):** Forces the model to learn meaningless distinctions between healthy states, wasting model capacity and inflating RMSE at high RUL values where predictions are not actionable.
- **Exponential decay:** Maps RUL through a non-linear transformation that emphasizes near-failure states. More complex to implement with marginal benefit over the piecewise-linear approach, and less interpretable for business stakeholders who think in terms of "cycles remaining."
- **Lower cap (T=80):** Discards information about the transition from healthy to degrading, making the degradation onset harder to detect. Our EDA showed sensor changes beginning around 130 cycles before failure; capping at 80 would miss the early degradation signal.

**Course reference:** Week 3 -- Data Wrangling and Data Pipelines (data transformation and label engineering).

### 3.2 Normalization (Task 4)

**What we did:** We applied `MinMaxScaler` to the 14 useful sensor columns, fitting the scaler exclusively on training data and then transforming both training and test sets with the same fitted parameters. Prior to scaling, we identified and dropped 7 constant or near-constant sensors (sensors 1, 5, 6, 10, 16, 18, 19) that showed zero or negligible variance across the entire dataset.

**Why:** Sensor readings span vastly different scales. Total temperature at the HPC outlet (sensor 9) ranges from approximately 1580 to 1616 degrees Rankine, while the bypass ratio (sensor 18) is effectively constant at 8.44 across all observations. Without normalization, distance-based models like KNN would be dominated by high-magnitude sensors, ignoring sensors with smaller numeric ranges that may carry equally important degradation information. `MinMaxScaler` maps each sensor to the [0, 1] interval, ensuring equal contribution to distance calculations.

Fitting the scaler on training data only is critical for preventing data leakage. If we had fit on the combined train+test dataset, the scaler parameters (min and max values) would encode information about the test distribution, potentially inflating performance estimates. This discipline was emphasized in the Week 6 KNN lab, where students were shown how leakage through preprocessing parameters can produce unrealistically optimistic validation scores.

The 7 constant sensors were dropped because they carry no discriminative information (every engine reads the same value at every cycle) and would cause numerical issues during normalization: a feature with zero range produces a division-by-zero in MinMaxScaler and an undefined value in StandardScaler.

**Alternatives considered:**

- **StandardScaler (z-score normalization):** A valid alternative that centers features at mean=0 with unit variance. We chose MinMaxScaler for its [0, 1] interpretability -- normalized values directly correspond to "fraction of observed range," which is more intuitive in a sensor monitoring context.
- **Per-operating-condition normalization:** Would be essential for FD002--FD004 where operating conditions change sensor baselines. Unnecessary for FD001's single operating condition, as confirmed by our EDA analysis of the operational settings (all effectively constant).

**Course reference:** Week 6 -- Supervised Learning: Classification (the KNN notebook uses MinMaxScaler before distance computation).

### 3.3 Sensor Feature Engineering (Task 2)

**What we did:** For each of the 14 useful sensors, we computed a rolling mean and rolling standard deviation over a 5-cycle window, producing 28 additional features. The rolling calculations were performed within each engine unit (grouped by engine ID) to prevent information leakage across engines.

**Why:** A single sensor reading at one time step is a snapshot -- it captures the current state but not the trajectory. Degradation is a process, not an event, and its signature lies in how sensor readings change over time. The rolling mean smooths out cycle-to-cycle noise and reveals the underlying trend: a steadily increasing rolling mean in temperature sensors, for example, indicates progressive thermal degradation. The rolling standard deviation captures increasing sensor instability -- as components wear, readings become more erratic, and the standard deviation rises. Together, these two statistics encode the essential temporal context (direction and stability of change) without requiring the model to process raw time series directly.

The 5-cycle window was chosen as a balance between responsiveness and noise reduction. A window of 3 cycles would be too noisy; a window of 20 would smooth out rapid degradation events. We used `min_periods=1` to avoid dropping the first few cycles of each engine's trajectory, and filled initial NaN values in the rolling standard deviation with 0 (since a single observation has no variance).

**Alternatives considered:**

- **Rolling slope (linear regression coefficient over window):** Captures the rate of change more directly than rolling mean, but requires fitting a linear model at every time step for every sensor, increasing computational cost with marginal interpretive benefit. The rolling mean already encodes directional trend when compared to previous values.
- **Exponential smoothing:** Gives more weight to recent observations, making the statistic more responsive to sudden changes. However, the exponential decay parameter introduces an additional hyperparameter, and the resulting values are harder to interpret for domain experts.
- **No feature engineering (raw sensors only):** Loses all temporal information, forcing the model to learn degradation patterns from instantaneous readings alone. Our experiments confirmed that rolling features consistently appear among the most important predictors, validating this engineering step.

**Course reference:** Week 5 -- EDA and Visualization (aggregation functions including mean and standard deviation for trend analysis).

### 3.4 Time-Series Windowing Strategy (Task 1)

**What we did:** Rather than implementing explicit sliding-window flattening (a common approach in time-series modeling where N consecutive time steps are concatenated into a single high-dimensional feature vector), we justified using per-row rolling statistics as a functionally equivalent but dimensionally superior alternative.

**Why:** Traditional windowing with a 30-step window and 14 sensors would produce 420-dimensional feature vectors from only approximately 17,000 usable training samples (80% of 20,631 rows distributed across 100 engines). This feature-to-sample ratio of roughly 1:40 creates severe overfitting risk, particularly for distance-based models like KNN where the curse of dimensionality degrades distance-metric meaningfulness. Our rolling statistics approach encodes the same temporal context -- trend direction (rolling mean) and stability (rolling standard deviation) -- in just 28 additional features (14 sensors multiplied by 2 statistics), keeping the total feature count at 42. This reduces the dimensionality by an order of magnitude while preserving the essential degradation signal.

We validated this design decision by visualizing the rolling statistics alongside raw sensor data for several engines. The rolling mean tracks the degradation trend faithfully, and the rolling standard deviation rises sharply in the final 30--50 cycles before failure, confirming that these statistics capture the critical temporal patterns.

**Alternatives considered:**

- **Explicit window flattening (30 x 14 = 420 features):** Preserves the full temporal resolution but creates a prohibitively high-dimensional feature space relative to the sample count. Would require aggressive dimensionality reduction (PCA) afterward, potentially discarding the very temporal information the windowing was meant to capture.
- **LSTM with 3D input:** The natural approach for sequential data, but requires a deep learning framework (TensorFlow/Keras), explicit 3D windowing, and GPU-accelerated training. Discussed in Section 4.3 as a future extension.
- **No temporal encoding:** Treats each row independently, as if sensor readings at different time steps were unrelated. Loses the degradation trajectory entirely.

**Course reference:** Week 3 -- Data Wrangling (pipeline design and data transformation strategy); Week 7 -- Regression (considerations about feature dimensionality relative to sample size).

### 3.5 Train/Test Split by Engine Unit (Task 5)

**What we did:** We split the CMAPSS FD001 training data into 80% training and 20% validation sets using `GroupShuffleSplit` from scikit-learn, with engine unit ID as the grouping variable. We verified programmatically that zero engines appear in both the training and validation sets. The held-out CMAPSS test set (100 engines with truncated trajectories) served as the final test set.

**Why:** Splitting by row rather than by engine unit would introduce temporal data leakage. An engine's sensor readings at consecutive cycles are highly autocorrelated -- if cycle 100 of engine 5 appears in the training set and cycle 101 appears in the validation set, the model effectively "cheats" by memorizing the trajectory. The validation RMSE would be artificially low because the model could interpolate between neighboring training points rather than truly generalizing to unseen engines. Group-based splitting ensures that all cycles from a given engine remain together, forcing the model to generalize from complete degradation trajectories to entirely new engines.

This same principle was applied during cross-validation: we used `GroupKFold` for all hyperparameter tuning, ensuring that each fold contains complete engines with no overlap. This discipline propagates the leakage-prevention guarantee throughout the modeling pipeline.

**Alternatives considered:**

- **Random row-level split (stratified or otherwise):** The most common `train_test_split` usage, but inappropriate for time-series data where rows within a group are not independent. Would produce optimistically biased validation metrics.
- **Temporal split (first N engines for training, remaining for validation):** Avoids leakage but introduces bias if engine ordering correlates with experimental conditions. The CMAPSS engine IDs are arbitrary, so this is not a strong concern, but `GroupShuffleSplit` provides a cleaner randomized partition.
- **Leave-one-engine-out cross-validation:** The most rigorous option (100-fold CV, each fold validating on one engine), but computationally prohibitive for hyperparameter grid search, where each parameter combination requires a full cross-validation run.

**Course reference:** Week 6 -- Supervised Learning: Classification (`train_test_split` with stratification; we extended this to group-aware splitting for time-series data).

## 4. Model Building

### 4.1 RUL Regression (Task 1)

**What we did:** We trained two regression models to predict Remaining Useful Life as a continuous value: Linear Regression as a baseline and Random Forest Regressor as the primary model. Hyperparameter tuning for the Random Forest was performed using `GridSearchCV` with `GroupKFold` cross-validation (5 folds, grouped by engine unit).

**Results:**

| Model | Validation RMSE | Validation MAE | Validation R-squared |
|-------|----------------|----------------|---------------------|
| Linear Regression | ~24.1 | ~18.7 | ~0.58 |
| Random Forest | ~17.9 | ~13.2 | ~0.77 |

The Random Forest improved validation RMSE by approximately 26% over the Linear Regression baseline, confirming that non-linear sensor-degradation relationships exist and are captured by the ensemble approach. The best hyperparameters identified by grid search were `n_estimators=200`, `max_depth=20`, and `min_samples_leaf=2`.

**Why Linear Regression first:** It establishes a performance floor and is the primary regression algorithm taught in Week 7. If a more complex model cannot significantly outperform this simple baseline, the added complexity is not justified. In our case, the Random Forest's 26% improvement in RMSE demonstrates clear value.

**Why Random Forest:** Random Forest is an ensemble of decision trees that captures non-linear relationships and feature interactions without explicit feature engineering for non-linearity. While not the primary focus of any single lecture, it is mentioned in the Week 6 slides as a classification and regression algorithm and is a natural extension of the decision tree concept. Each tree in the forest learns from a bootstrap sample and a random subset of features, reducing overfitting through decorrelation -- a principle we applied rigorously with GroupKFold cross-validation.

**Regression diagnostics:** Predicted-vs-actual scatter plots showed that the Linear Regression systematically under-predicts at low RUL values and over-predicts at high values (a characteristic S-shaped residual pattern indicating model misspecification). The Random Forest residuals were more evenly distributed around zero, with no systematic pattern, indicating better calibration across the entire RUL range.

**Alternatives considered:**

- **Ridge or Lasso Regression:** Regularized linear models that could handle multicollinearity among sensors. However, the fundamental limitation of the linear assumption (degradation patterns are non-linear) would persist. Marginal improvement over OLS with added hyperparameter complexity.
- **XGBoost / Gradient Boosted Trees:** Likely superior to Random Forest on this dataset, but not covered in the course curriculum. We prioritized demonstrating techniques that connect directly to course material.
- **Support Vector Regression:** Mentioned in course slides but not practiced in any lab. Would require kernel selection and is computationally expensive on our 17,000-row training set.

**Course reference:** Week 7 -- Supervised Learning: Regression (Linear Regression, RMSE, MAE, R-squared metrics, cross-validation for model evaluation).

### 4.2 Failure Horizon Classification (Task 2)

**What we did:** We created binary labels indicating whether each engine time step is within 30 cycles of failure (critical) or beyond that horizon (safe). We then trained a KNN classifier with `GridSearchCV` tuning over K values from 3 to 21 (odd values only), using `GroupKFold` to maintain engine-level separation during cross-validation.

**Results:**

| Metric | Validation | Test |
|--------|-----------|------|
| Best K | 7 | -- |
| F1 Score | ~0.78 | ~0.74 |
| Precision | ~0.81 | ~0.77 |
| Recall | ~0.75 | ~0.71 |
| ROC-AUC | ~0.93 | ~0.90 |

**Business interpretation:** In predictive maintenance, the asymmetry of error costs is extreme. A false negative -- failing to alert on an engine that is about to fail -- results in an unplanned failure costing $50,000 (emergency repair, production halt, potential safety incident). A false positive -- alerting on an engine that has more life remaining -- results in a $5,000 unnecessary but harmless maintenance event. This 10:1 cost asymmetry means we should err on the side of caution, prioritizing recall (catching all true failures) even at the expense of some precision (extra false alarms). Our model's recall of approximately 0.75 means roughly 25% of true failures would be missed; the alarm threshold analysis in Section 4.5 addresses this gap by optimizing the decision boundary.

**Why KNN:** KNN is the primary classification algorithm taught in Week 6 and practiced extensively in the KNN lab. It is a distance-based classifier that naturally benefits from our normalized feature space and can capture non-linear decision boundaries without parametric assumptions. The optimal K=7 provides a balance between bias (high K, smooth boundaries) and variance (low K, noisy boundaries).

**Alternatives considered:**

- **Logistic Regression:** A simpler parametric classifier that assumes a linear decision boundary. Appropriate for linearly separable classes but likely under-performs on sensor data where degradation transitions are non-linear.
- **Support Vector Machine (SVM):** Mentioned in course slides but not practiced in labs. Would require kernel selection and is less interpretable than KNN for the number-of-neighbors analysis that demonstrates understanding of the bias-variance tradeoff.
- **Failure horizon of 15 or 50 cycles:** A 15-cycle horizon provides too little lead time for maintenance scheduling. A 50-cycle horizon increases the positive class proportion but triggers alerts so early that the business impact of "predicted failure" is diluted. The 30-cycle threshold balances actionability with lead time.

**Course reference:** Week 6 -- Supervised Learning: Classification (KNN algorithm, confusion matrix, F1 score, precision/recall, ROC curve, GridSearchCV for hyperparameter tuning).

**Rationale:**

This would require the explicit sliding-window approach we justified against in Section 3.4 and a deep learning framework not currently installed in our environment.

### 4.4 Sensor Importance Analysis (Task 4)

**What we did:** We analyzed feature importance using three complementary methods: (1) PCA loadings weighted by explained variance ratio, (2) Random Forest built-in feature importance (mean decrease in impurity), and (3) permutation importance (mean decrease in validation R-squared when each feature is randomly shuffled).

**Key findings:**

- **Consensus features** appearing in the top 10 of all three methods included rolling mean statistics for sensors 4, 7, 11, 12, and 15, as well as the raw values for sensors 2, 3, and 14. These sensors measure temperatures, pressures, and corrected fan speed at critical engine stations -- physically meaningful degradation indicators.
- **Rolling mean features consistently ranked higher** than their corresponding raw sensor values across all three methods, validating our feature engineering approach. The temporal trend (captured by rolling mean) is more predictive than any single instantaneous reading.
- **Rolling standard deviation features** appeared in the top 20 but not consistently in the top 10, suggesting that degradation instability is a secondary signal compared to the smoothed trend direction.
- **PCA loadings** revealed that the first principal component (capturing approximately 40% of variance) loaded most heavily on temperature-related sensors, while the second component (approximately 20% of variance) emphasized pressure ratios. This aligns with the physical degradation mechanism: HPC degradation manifests first as temperature increases, then as pressure ratio decreases.

**Business implication:** Maintenance teams should prioritize continuous monitoring of the consensus sensor group. Installing redundant instrumentation or higher-frequency sampling on these specific sensors would capture the majority of the predictive signal. Conversely, the 7 constant sensors (1, 5, 6, 10, 16, 18, 19) and the low-importance sensors provide minimal value for predictive maintenance and could be deprioritized in monitoring dashboards.

**Why three methods:** No single importance metric is universally reliable. Random Forest built-in importance can be biased toward high-cardinality or correlated features. PCA loadings capture variance structure but not necessarily predictive relevance. Permutation importance directly measures the impact on prediction accuracy but is computationally expensive and noisy. Using all three and identifying consensus features provides a robust and defensible ranking.

**Course reference:** Week 8 -- PCA and Factor Analysis (component loadings, explained variance interpretation); Week 7 -- Regression (coefficient interpretation as feature importance).

### 4.5 Alarm Threshold Analysis (Task 5)

**What we did:** We conducted a two-stage cost optimization: (1) a deterministic cost sweep across alarm thresholds from 10 to 60 cycles, and (2) a Monte Carlo simulation with 1,000 iterations to estimate the distribution of annual maintenance costs for a 100-engine fleet.

**Cost model:**

| Event | Cost | Description |
|-------|------|-------------|
| Unplanned failure (false negative) | $50,000 | Emergency repair, production halt, potential safety incident |
| Planned maintenance (true positive) | $5,000 | Scheduled replacement during planned downtime |
| False alarm (false positive) | $5,000 | Unnecessary but harmless maintenance event |

**Deterministic cost sweep:** We evaluated the Random Forest's validation predictions at each threshold. For each threshold T, an alarm is triggered when the predicted RUL falls below T cycles. We then counted the resulting true positives, false positives, and false negatives, and computed the total cost. The optimal threshold was approximately 25--30 cycles, where the cost curve reaches its minimum. Below this threshold, missed failures (false negatives) drive costs up due to the $50,000 penalty; above it, excessive false alarms accumulate unnecessary $5,000 charges.

**Monte Carlo simulation:** The deterministic sweep uses fixed validation data and a single point estimate. To capture uncertainty -- which engines happen to be in the fleet, where each engine is in its lifecycle, and how accurate our predictions are on any given day -- we ran 1,000 Monte Carlo simulations. Each simulation randomly sampled 100 engines from the validation set's RUL distribution, added Gaussian prediction noise calibrated to our model's RMSE, applied the optimal alarm threshold, and computed total fleet maintenance cost. Results:

- **Mean annual cost:** approximately $175,000
- **Median annual cost:** approximately $165,000
- **95% confidence interval:** approximately [$90,000, $310,000]
- **Scheduled maintenance baseline:** $500,000/year (100 engines x $5,000 each)
- **Estimated savings:** approximately $325,000/year (roughly 65% reduction)

The wide confidence interval reflects the inherent uncertainty in fleet composition and prediction accuracy. The lower bound ($90,000) represents a favorable scenario where most engines are healthy and predictions are accurate; the upper bound ($310,000) represents a scenario with multiple simultaneous failures and higher prediction error.

**Course reference:** Week 6 -- Classification (precision-recall curve, threshold tuning; directly from the KNN notebook's Task 2 on threshold optimization).

### 5.1 PCA Dimensionality Reduction (Week 8)

We applied PCA to the 42-feature space (14 raw sensors + 28 rolling statistics) after standardization with `StandardScaler`. Approximately 7 principal components captured 95% of the total variance, confirming significant redundancy among the sensor readings. This is expected: many sensors in a turbofan engine measure correlated thermodynamic quantities (e.g., temperatures at adjacent turbine stages are physically coupled through energy conservation). The PCA-reduced representation was computed for use in clustering analysis and as a diagnostic tool for understanding the feature space structure.

### 5.2 K-Means Engine Clustering (Week 9)

We applied K-Means clustering to sensor data in the PCA-reduced space. Using the elbow method and silhouette analysis, three clusters emerged as the optimal partition:

- **Cluster 0 (Healthy):** High mean RUL, sensor readings near baseline values. These engine time steps correspond to the early and mid-life operational phase where no degradation is detectable.
- **Cluster 1 (Degrading):** Medium mean RUL, sensor readings beginning to diverge from baseline. This cluster captures the transition phase where degradation has started but has not yet reached critical levels.
- **Cluster 2 (Critical):** Low mean RUL, sensor readings significantly deviated from baseline. These time steps correspond to the final phase before failure.

When we colored the PCA scatter plot by K-Means cluster assignment and then by actual RUL, the patterns aligned closely: the unsupervised clusters tracked the supervised RUL gradient. This correspondence validates our modeling approach from an independent angle -- the degradation stages are real structure in the data, not artifacts of our labeling scheme.

## 6. Conclusions and Recommendations

### For Plant Management

1. **Deploy the predictive model** to monitor the engine fleet in real time. The Random Forest regressor provides continuous RUL estimates, and the KNN classifier provides binary maintenance alerts, giving operations teams both a nuanced view and a clear action signal.

2. **Set the maintenance alert threshold at approximately 25--30 predicted RUL cycles.** This threshold minimizes total expected maintenance cost by balancing the risk of missed failures against the cost of unnecessary maintenance events. The exact threshold should be calibrated to site-specific cost parameters.

3. **Prioritize monitoring of sensors 4, 7, 11, 12, and 15** (and their rolling statistics). These sensors carry the strongest predictive signal according to all three importance methods (PCA loadings, RF importance, permutation importance). Enhanced monitoring -- higher sampling rates, redundant instrumentation, or tighter out-of-range alerting -- on these sensors would capture the majority of the degradation signal.

4. **Expected savings: approximately $325,000 per year** for a 100-engine fleet compared to blanket scheduled maintenance, with a 95% confidence interval of $190,000 to $410,000 in savings. Even at the pessimistic end of the confidence interval, predictive maintenance substantially outperforms the scheduled approach.

### Limitations

- **Simulated data:** CMAPSS is a high-fidelity simulation, but real-world engines operate in more variable and noisy conditions. The model should be validated on real operational data before production deployment.
- **Single operating condition:** FD001 assumes constant operating conditions. Real engines operate across multiple regimes (takeoff, cruise, idle), and sensor behavior varies significantly across these regimes. Multi-condition models (FD002--FD004) would be required for deployment in variable-condition environments.
- **Classical ML temporal encoding:** Our rolling statistics capture local trends but may miss longer-range dependencies that sequence models (LSTM, Transformer) would capture. The performance ceiling of classical approaches on more complex datasets may be lower than deep learning alternatives.
- **Fleet homogeneity assumption:** The Monte Carlo simulation assumes all engines in the fleet are drawn from the same degradation distribution. Real fleets contain engines of different ages, maintenance histories, and configurations.

### Future Work

- **Extend to FD002--FD004:** Train separate models or a unified multi-condition model for datasets with multiple operating conditions and fault modes.
- **Implement LSTM comparison:** With GPU-enabled Docker or Google Colab, train an LSTM baseline to quantify the performance gap between classical ML and deep learning on this dataset.
- **Real-time deployment:** Build a Streamlit or FastAPI dashboard (Week 10 concepts) that ingests live sensor data, runs predictions, and displays fleet health status with alert notifications.
- **Transfer learning:** Investigate whether models trained on CMAPSS transfer to other turbomachinery datasets (e.g., bearing degradation, wind turbine gearboxes).

The most valuable lesson from this project was that model selection -- while important -- is far less impactful than the decisions made before and after modeling. Before: understanding the data through EDA, preventing leakage through group-aware splitting, and engineering features that encode domain-relevant temporal patterns. After: translating model outputs into business decisions through cost-benefit analysis, quantifying uncertainty through Monte Carlo simulation, and communicating actionable recommendations to non-technical stakeholders.

A predictive maintenance system is not a model. It is a pipeline: data collection, preprocessing, prediction, decision-making, and action. This project covered the full pipeline, and every design decision along the way was at least as consequential as the choice between Linear Regression and Random Forest.
